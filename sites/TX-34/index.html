Navigation for people who walk.

The spark

Pedestrian mode is broken.

Plenty of people in my city walk — to jobs, to school, to the store. A lot of them walk on highway shoulders in 100°F heat because that's what the maps tell them to do.

Apple Maps, Google Maps, and Waze all have a "pedestrian" mode, but it's not really pedestrian. They push you down wide, straight arterials with few turns — because that's what car routing prefers underneath. The architecture is car-centric; walking just inherits the bias.

Maps optimize for cars. Even the walking directions do — they're just car routes with a slower number on them.

So I built Heatwave. It's the map I wanted to exist.

How it works

A map that actually gets walking.

Heatwave re-scores routes for people on foot. Instead of the shortest or fastest path, it picks the one that actually feels walkable: shadier, quieter, safer.

Shade-aware

Routes get scored on tree canopy and building shadow at the time you're walking. Not just how long the line is.

Safety-first

Skips high-speed arterials without sidewalks — the kind every other map sends you down anyway.

Pedestrian logic

Built on OSM walking data from the ground up. Feet get their own graph, not a knockoff of the driver one.

7,000+ routes scored nationwide so far
1st pedestrian thermal model of its kind — built from scratch, by yours truly
About me
